# General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

Compact compliance-engine summary of key GDPR provisions for website compliance.

## Art. 6 — Lawfulness of processing

**Core content:** Processing personal data is lawful only if at least one of six legal bases applies: consent, contract performance, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests. Controllers must identify and document the applicable legal basis before processing.

**Website compliance relevance:** Websites need a lawful basis for analytics, forms, newsletters, user accounts, tracking, and other data processing. Consent is commonly required for non-essential cookies and tracking, while contract or legitimate interests may apply to strictly necessary services.

## Art. 7 — Conditions for consent

**Core content:** Where processing is based on consent, the controller must be able to demonstrate that the data subject consented. Consent requests must be clear, distinguishable, easy to withdraw, and not bundled with unrelated terms.

**Website compliance relevance:** Cookie banners, newsletter opt-ins, and marketing permissions must use clear affirmative action and allow withdrawal as easily as consent was given. Pre-ticked boxes or hidden consent mechanisms are not compliant.

## Art. 12 — Transparent information

**Core content:** Controllers must provide information about processing in a concise, transparent, intelligible, and easily accessible form using clear and plain language. Responses to data subject rights requests must be handled within the required timeframes.

**Website compliance relevance:** Privacy notices and consent interfaces must be readable, accessible, and easy to find. Legal information should not be buried in vague or overly complex text.

## Art. 13 — Information to be provided where personal data are collected from the data subject

**Core content:** When personal data are collected directly from users, controllers must provide identity and contact details, purposes, legal bases, recipients, retention periods, rights, complaint options, and other required information. The notice must be provided at the time of collection.

**Website compliance relevance:** Websites collecting data through forms, accounts, cookies, or tracking must provide a complete privacy notice before or during collection. Missing controller details, legal bases, retention periods, or user rights create compliance risks.

## Art. 17 — Right to erasure

**Core content:** Data subjects have the right to obtain deletion of personal data in defined cases, including when the data are no longer necessary, consent is withdrawn, or processing is unlawful. Exceptions apply, for example for legal obligations or legal claims.

**Website compliance relevance:** Websites should provide clear deletion request channels and backend workflows to erase account, lead, newsletter, or tracking data where required. Privacy notices should explain how users can exercise this right.

## Art. 77 — Right to lodge a complaint with a supervisory authority

**Core content:** Data subjects may lodge a complaint with a supervisory authority if they consider that processing of their personal data infringes the GDPR. This right exists without prejudice to other administrative or judicial remedies.

**Website compliance relevance:** Privacy notices should inform users about their complaint right and identify the competent or relevant supervisory authority. Missing complaint information is a common privacy notice defect.
